No Man's Sky has 256 galaxies. Most players never leave the first one. That's not a dig — Euclid is enormous and you could sink a thousand hours in it without running out of things to do. But if you want to go further, this is how it works.

---

## The basics: how galaxies work

Each galaxy in No Man's Sky contains billions of procedurally generated star systems. You start in galaxy 1, **Euclid**, and can travel through them in order by flying to the Galaxy Centre. The sequence runs from 1 to 255, then loops back to Euclid. Galaxy 256 exists in the data but is unreachable through normal play.

Every galaxy has its own name, its own color palette at the core, and its own regional naming conventions for star systems and regions. The underlying game mechanics — resources, crafting, progression — are identical across all of them. You're not unlocking new content by galaxy-hopping. You're exploring different procedural seeds.

### The four galaxy types

Galaxies fall into one of four types, which affects planet generation and survival difficulty:

Norm is the default — 178 of the 256 galaxies fall here, shown with a cyan hologram and labeled Imperfect, Improved, Parallel, or Rebuilt depending on which one you're in. Standard planet distribution, standard survival conditions. Nothing unusual.

Lush galaxies (green hologram, 25 total) go by names like Halcyon, Inspiring, Serene, or Tranquil. They produce a noticeably higher proportion of green and blue planets with milder weather. If you're into base building or just want somewhere that doesn't actively try to kill you, Lush is the type you're targeting.

Harsh galaxies (red hologram, 26 total) are Burning, Raging, Relentless, or Ruthless. More extreme weather, more toxic and irradiated worlds. The difficulty is real, but the visual aesthetic tends toward dramatic. Some players love it.

Empty galaxies (blue hologram, 26 total) are Ancestral, Frozen, Exhausted, or Silent. Fewer star systems, sparser everything. Quiet in a way that feels deliberate. A niche choice, but people do seek them out.

The type only governs planet generation tendencies — it doesn't change what resources exist or lock any mechanics.

## What actually changes between galaxies

Less than you might expect. Your ship, exosuit, multi-tool, units, nanites, and all inventory contents come with you. Blueprints carry over. Faction reputation carries over. Your base teleporters work across galaxies.

What changes:

- Planet generation shifts. Lush galaxies genuinely produce more hospitable worlds. Harsh galaxies lean into extremes.
- Regional naming conventions are different per galaxy. You'll notice the suffix and prefix patterns on star system names change.
- The galaxy core color changes — Euclid is yellow, Eissentam is orange, Hilbert Dimension is blue-green. Purely cosmetic, but a nice visual confirmation that you've actually arrived somewhere new.
- Community presence drops sharply after Euclid and Eissentam. If you rely on the Nexus for group missions or trading, expect less activity the deeper you go.

What doesn't change: the resource pool, crafting recipes, tech trees, or any core game system. Galaxy 200 plays identically to Galaxy 1 from a mechanical standpoint.

## How to reach the Galaxy Centre

This is the main method of galaxy travel and the one with the most friction.

### Step 1: Navigate to the center

Open the Galactic Map and select "Galactic Centre" as your destination. A route appears. The issue is distance — the center can be tens of thousands of light-years away from wherever you are in the galaxy, and each Hyperdrive jump covers 1,000–2,500 LY depending on your drive and upgrades. Warping there manually would take hours.

The solution is black holes.

### Step 2: Use black holes to skip distance

Black holes are spatial anomalies that fling you much closer to the galactic center in a single jump. They skip thousands of light-years instantly. The downside is they damage one random piece of technology in your ship each time you use one.

To find a black hole, talk to **Polo** at the Space Anomaly. Ask about black hole locations and one will appear on your Galactic Map. From that point you can chain black hole jumps — each one gets you closer — until you're in range of the center itself.

### Step 3: Make the final jump

Once you're within range of the core (a few thousand light-years), the Galactic Map will let you select the Galaxy Centre directly. Your Hyperdrive must be fully charged. Hold the selection until it activates, and you're gone.

You arrive in the next galaxy with a crash landing on a random planet. All installed technology across your ship, exosuit, and multi-tool breaks and needs repairing. Bring repair kits.

### What you need before you go

- Fully charged Hyperdrive
- A stockpile of Repair Kits (you'll need several on arrival)
- A base left behind in your current galaxy if you ever want to return
- Ideally: a cheap ship you don't mind sacrificing to the repair cycle. Many players keep a junk shuttle specifically for galaxy transitions.

## The Artemis Path skip

Finishing the main story — specifically the **New Beginnings** mission at the end of the Artemis Path — gives you a one-time galaxy choice. You pick a galaxy type (Norm, Lush, Harsh, or Empty) and jump directly to the nearest galaxy of that type in sequence, potentially skipping several galaxies at once.

If you want Eissentam (Lush, galaxy 10) and you're still in Euclid, this is the fastest legitimate way to get there. Complete the story, choose Lush, and you'll arrive in Eissentam regardless of how far you were from galaxy 10 in the normal sequence.

This skip only works once. After that, Galaxy Centre travel is the method.

## How to travel back to a previous galaxy

Here's the piece most guides bury: you can go back, and it's not hard.

The teleporter network ignores galaxy boundaries. If you left a base in galaxy 3 and you're now in galaxy 15, you can open any base teleporter and jump back to galaxy 3 instantly. Tech doesn't break. You arrive next to your base.

This is why you should always drop a Base Computer in every new galaxy you enter, even if you never build anything on it. The Base Computer is the anchor. Without it, the only way back is another Galaxy Centre run.

The Space Anomaly teleporter works the same way — you can warp to any previously visited player base in any galaxy via the Nexus. If a friend has a base in Eissentam and you're still in Euclid, you can visit it directly.

## Notable galaxies

Most of the 255 galaxies are interchangeable for practical purposes. A few stand out for specific reasons.

Euclid (1) is where everyone starts. The largest active player community by a wide margin. The Nexus has the most traffic here, which matters for group missions and trading.

Hilbert Dimension (2) is the first stop for anyone doing a proper galaxy run. Norm type, blue-green core. More of a milestone than a destination — people go there to say they did.

Calypso (3) is Harsh. Worth a look if you want a more punishing environment without committing to a full hostile-galaxy run.

Eissentam (10) is the most popular destination after Euclid, and for good reason. It's Lush — genuinely better odds of green planets, blue oceans, calm weather. The player community is smaller than Euclid but more focused on exploration and building. If you want to end up there, the Artemis Path Lush skip is the fastest route.

Beyond Eissentam, you're mostly in completionist territory. The galaxies keep coming but the practical differences thin out fast.

## Tips for galaxy hopping efficiently

A few things that make the process less painful.

Chain black holes. Each one skips thousands of light-years. Five to eight jumps will get you within striking distance of any galaxy center far faster than warping manually. The tech damage is annoying but fixable — don't let it stop you.

Always drop a base before leaving. Takes 30 seconds, costs almost nothing, and it's the only way to teleport back later. Don't skip this.

Carry Repair Kits. You need them immediately on arrival. Craft them from Di-hydrogen Jelly and Metal Plating before you go — make more than you think you need.

Travel with a secondary ship if you can. Keep a cheap hauler for galaxy transitions and let it eat the tech damage. Repair it, then switch back to your main ship at the Space Station.

Portal coordinates work in every galaxy. Once you have all 16 glyphs, you can portal to specific system coordinates anywhere. The address `100104005005` lands you in a system very close to most galactic centers, cutting the final approach to almost nothing.

## The galaxy loop

After Iousongola, galaxy 255, the sequence ends and loops back to Euclid. You arrive in the first galaxy again as if you'd completed a circuit. Some players treat this as a long-term goal — run the full 255. It's a commitment measured in hundreds of hours, but the community for it exists, and there are documented records of players who've done it.

Two galaxies — Yilsrussimil and the post-loop Odyalutai — are no longer reachable through normal play following the Atlas Rises update. They existed early in the game's life but were closed off. The only way in now is joining a multiplayer session with someone already there, or a save editor on PC.
