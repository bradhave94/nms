Cooking in No Man's Sky can become a rabbit hole fast, because the Nutrient Processor supports a huge number of combinations. The fastest way to get value from it is to focus on a few repeatable goals:

- **Cronus nanite turn-ins**
- **high-value food for selling**
- **bait and fishing support ingredients**
- **expedition or survival support items**

If you want the full recipe list, start with the [cooking recipe table](/cooking). If you want the most efficient route, use the priorities below.

## 1. Prioritise simple ingredient chains first

The best cooking recipes usually start with ingredients you can gather in bulk:

- milk, eggs, and creature products
- wheat, frost crystal, and plant-based ingredients
- fish and fish-derived components
- sugar, flour, butter, and pastry bases

These are strong because they let you build layered recipes without hunting rare one-off items every time.

For ingredient lookup, the [food database](/food) and [fish pages](/fish) are the quickest way to trace what each item can become.

## 2. Build processing staples before premium recipes

Before chasing fancy meals, stock up on the staples that appear in many outputs:

- processed sugar
- flour
- butter
- batter and pastry-style bases
- fish ingredients used in bait and seafood chains

This reduces friction because one gathering session can feed multiple cooking runs. It also makes it easier to pivot into whichever final recipe fits your current goal.

## 3. Best cooking recipes for nanite farming

If your goal is nanites through **Cronus**, choose recipes that:

- use ingredients you can farm repeatedly
- have multiple upgrade paths from the same base materials
- create higher-tier meals instead of basic snacks

The exact best recipe depends on what you produce most easily, but in general:

- creature farm setups are excellent for steady dairy and egg chains
- crop-heavy farms are excellent for flour, sugar, and dessert chains
- mixed farms give the most flexibility

The key is consistency. A recipe that is slightly less valuable but easy to repeat is usually better than a perfect recipe you can only make occasionally.

## 4. Best recipes for fishing support

Fishing added a new reason to care about cooking ingredients. If you are working on fish-related progression:

- keep seafood ingredients separate by biome and rarity
- turn excess catches into useful intermediates
- stock bait-related outputs that support longer fishing sessions

The [fish section](/fish) is especially useful here because many catches have value beyond selling. Some become stronger ingredients in later food chains, while others are more useful as support materials for repeat fishing.

## 5. Best recipes for credits

Cooking is not always the fastest credit farm compared with high-end crafting chains, but it can be a good supplemental loop when:

- you already collect lots of cooking ingredients passively
- you run creature ranching or crop farms
- you want a relaxed gameplay loop instead of industrial crafting

For pure credits, compare your options against [Products](/products) and the [Crafting Guide](/crafting-guide). If the goal is maximum profit, higher-end crafted products often win. If the goal is low-friction conversion of gathered materials, cooking stays attractive.

## 6. Use the Nutrient Processor like a production chain

The biggest cooking mistake is treating each recipe as an isolated action. Instead:

1. gather one family of ingredients in bulk
2. turn them into reusable processed staples
3. choose the highest-value final outputs from those staples
4. repeat the same profitable chain

That makes cooking feel much closer to a proper manufacturing loop instead of random experimentation.
